Today's small group exercises:

Look at your paper handout to see which exercise you and your tablemates have been assigned to. You only need to download that one exercise:

  • Opiate-related deaths
  • Police incidents
  • School test scores
  • Election results

    • Best practices:

    • Finding data: just use your reporting skills; ask a lot of questions; do requests and as many of the questions via email so you have a paper trail (keep it all!)
    • Try to get as broad of a dataset as you can -- more columns/more rows means more flexible and the higher number of questions you can ask (example of the distracted driving request)
    • Keep a copy of your original data
    • Expect that there will be problems with your data, inconsistencies, flaws, etc.; Go looking for them before they find you
    • Learn how to use a data-cleaning tool like Open Refine
    • Write a list of the questions you want to ask your data (just like you might prep for an interview)
    • Keep notes about your analysis
    • Repeat your analysis, start to finish, to make sure you didn’t miss anything
    • Try writing a “how I did it” and you’ll discover possible holes
    • If something in your results doesn’t quite seem right, it’s probably not
    • Recruit a colleague and talk through your findings/analysis with them. They might bring up things you hadn’t thought of
    • Use NICAR-L to ask questions when you get stuck
    • If it seems like whatever you're trying to do seems harder than it should be, there's probably a more efficient way to do it. Reach out to other data journalists for suggestions!
